01 — Origin

From Chiapas to the Colonial Archive

Frederick came to history the hard way. Before graduate school, before the Newberry Library, before any of the books, he worked as a wildland firefighter for the U.S. Forest Service. In 1998, a catastrophic outbreak of forest fires in southern Mexico led to his deployment to Chiapas, where he trained local crews in containment and suppression.

What he encountered there was not only ecological crisis but a set of deeper questions about how states respond to disaster, who bears the cost of catastrophe, and how those patterns recur across centuries. Those questions followed him to Penn State, where he earned his doctorate, and have informed every major project since.

His first book investigated how the Totonac communities of Papantla, Veracruz navigated the economic violence of the Bourbon tobacco monopoly. His second traced the entangled monetary foundations of two post-colonial republics. His current research, on firefighting in eighteenth-century Mexico, represents something like a return: the historian circling back to where the questions began.

At the oven. Always near the fire.

02 — Published Works

The Books

2016  •  Sussex Academic Press

Riot! Tobacco, Reform, and Violence in Eighteenth-Century Papantla, Mexico

ISBN 9781845198169

An examination of four uprisings in Papantla, Veracruz between 1736 and 1787. The Bourbon monopolization of tobacco in 1764 destabilized an already fragile colonial arrangement, and Frederick reconstructs how the Totonac community responded: through petition, negotiation, and, when those failed, organized violence. The book's central argument is that colonial rule in Papantla was constituted through ongoing negotiation, not imposed from above.

Colonial Mexico Indigenous Agency Economic Violence
2017  •  Rowman & Littlefield

Spanish Dollars and Sister Republics: The Money That Made Mexico and the United States

With Tatiana Seijas  •  ISBN 9781538100462

Both Mexico and the United States adopted the Spanish piece of eight as their monetary foundation. Seijas and Frederick follow that shared inheritance from the 1770s through the 1860s, tracing how each republic designed coinage to express national ideals, struggled to establish monetary legitimacy, and confronted the material realities of building an economy from colonial remnants.

04 — In the Classroom

Courses & Pedagogy

Frederick is, by his own description, "a fairly demanding professor." His seminars tend to favor primary sources over textbook summaries, and he structures discussion around the assumption that students are capable of more rigorous analysis than they've been asked to produce. The courses below reflect his core preoccupations: the exercise and collapse of imperial power, the construction of racial categories under colonialism, and the way catastrophic events expose the fault lines of the societies they strike.

05 — Current Work

The Fire Continues

Active Research

Fire and Control in Bourbon Mexico

Supported by a Newberry Library fellowship, Frederick is investigating how colonial authorities in eighteenth-century Mexico attempted to manage urban fire: the institutional arrangements, the labor regimes, the material infrastructure of prevention and suppression. It is, in a sense, the project his entire career has been building toward. The former firefighter, now deep in the archives, reading how an earlier empire tried to contain what he once fought by hand.
